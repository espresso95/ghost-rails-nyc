# BMT Brooklyn Notes

Brooklyn's elevated and rapid transit history includes both demolished elevated structures and smaller closed station remnants. These features are useful for showing how rapid transit lines were rerouted, rebuilt, removed, or absorbed into later subway service.

The Myrtle Avenue closed platform near DeKalb Avenue is tied to major Downtown Brooklyn track reconfiguration. It is best discussed as a closed platform feature visible only from lawful public or regular-train viewpoints where supported by sources.

Dean Street was a former Franklin Avenue Shuttle station. It closed in 1995 during the shuttle's reconstruction period. For app purposes, it is a good example of a relatively recent closed station where street-level context is safer than infrastructure access.

The Myrtle Avenue elevated once extended farther west than today's active M service. The western section was removed, while active elevated service remains farther east. The distinction between demolished, active, and remnant infrastructure should be clear in answers.

The Fulton Street Elevated was a Brooklyn elevated route that was removed as subway service patterns changed. It helps explain how older elevated corridors shaped later rapid transit geography.

Sands Street Terminal near the Brooklyn Bridge approach was a major elevated railway terminal in early Brooklyn rapid transit history. It no longer exists as transit infrastructure and should be presented as historical context.
