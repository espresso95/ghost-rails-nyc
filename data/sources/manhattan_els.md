# Manhattan Elevated Railways

Manhattan's former elevated railways are central to understanding the city before the modern subway network fully replaced earlier rapid transit patterns. The Second Avenue, Third Avenue, and Ninth Avenue elevated lines each help explain how transit routes shaped neighborhoods, street conditions, and later subway planning.

The Second Avenue Elevated served Manhattan's East Side before its removal. It is useful for comparing lost elevated service with the much later Second Avenue Subway. Answers should avoid implying that the old elevated and the modern subway are direct equivalents.

The Third Avenue Elevated was a major East Side elevated route. Manhattan service ended before the last Bronx portion disappeared. In Ghost Rails NYC, the Manhattan feature is mapped as a historical alignment and should be discussed from public street context.

The Ninth Avenue Elevated was one of Manhattan's earliest elevated routes. It helps explain the older elevated era and the shift toward subway operations.

Former elevated lines should be safe to discuss as demolished public-history layers. They should not be framed as restricted access targets because the structures no longer exist in their original form.
